03

Graffiti Artists
are Type Designers

My role: Editorial Design, Book Binding & Layout

A handbound editorial book arguing that graffiti artists and type designers share the same iterative process of critique, form development, and letterform mastery. Developed a torn-image layout language, acrylic-painted covers, and screw-post binding to bridge street energy with typographic craft.

Timeline6 weeks
ToolsInDesign, Photoshop, Illustrator
InstructorKelcey Gray
CourseTypography 1, UT Austin

Project Brief

For Typography 1, I was asked to create a book on any topic related to type. I chose to explore the intersection of graffiti and typography — a connection I’d been curious about since childhood. The central argument: graffiti artists are type designers. Both develop letterforms through iteration, critique, and a deep attention to form.

Research

I researched the historical and cultural roots of graffiti — how it emerged as a voice for marginalized communities, evolved its own style taxonomy (tags, throw-ups, wildstyle), and developed a culture of mentorship and critique that mirrors formal type education. I drew parallels between a graffiti writer refining a tag over years and a type designer iterating on a letterform across weights and optical sizes.

Layout Ideation

My first layouts felt too clean — like a textbook, not a book about street art. The turning point came on a walk through campus: torn flyers layered on top of each other on a bulletin board. That chaotic, layered energy was exactly what I needed. I developed a torn-image technique — ripping printed photos by hand and scanning the raw edges — to give every spread the feeling of peeling back layers of a city wall.

“The torn edges became the visual language of the whole book — a bridge between the raw energy of graffiti and the precision of typographic layout.”

Prototyping & Materials

The physical form had to match the content. I painted the covers with acrylic to echo the texture of spray paint on concrete. The spine is wrapped in book fabric, and the pages are held with screw-post binding — a nod to the modular, additive nature of both graffiti and type design. Each material choice reinforced the argument that these two worlds share more than they divide.

Final Book

The finished book balances the chaotic energy of graffiti with the readability of a designed editorial piece. Torn photographic elements sit alongside clean typographic hierarchies. The pacing moves from historical context to artist profiles to formal analysis — guiding the reader through the argument while keeping the visual intensity high.

What I Learned

This project taught me how material and form can carry an argument as strongly as words. The torn-image technique, the painted covers, the binding method — each decision reinforced the thesis. I also learned that the best design solutions often come from stepping away from the screen and observing the world around you.

“Both graffiti writers and type designers spend years refining the same 26 letters — the difference is the surface, not the obsession.”
